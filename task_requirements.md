## 5.2 Survey Design and Data Collection

### 5.2.1 Define your objective and target audience

#### 5.2.1.1 Clearly explain what you are trying to learn from your survey

#### 5.2.1.2 Clearly explain from whom you are trying to obtain the data

##### 5.2.1.2.1 After the broad population is defined, tell me your sample

A sample is a subset of your target audience who will actually take the survey.

---

### 5.2.2 Survey method

#### 5.2.2.1 Define your method

##### 5.2.2.1.1 Make sure it matches your target audience, stated objective, and available resources

##### 5.2.2.1.2 Examples

Examples include, but are not limited to:

- Online surveys
- Telephone and face-to-face surveys
- Panel surveys

---

### 5.2.3 Questionnaire Design

#### 5.2.3.1 Question types used

##### 5.2.3.1.1 Minimum 2 question types

Examples:

- Multiple choice
- Rankings
- Open-ended responses
- Likert scale
- Matrix
- Slider
- Ordinal scale

#### 5.2.3.1.2 Minimum 7 questions

##### 5.2.3.1.2.1 All questions should be clear, direct, and without bias

Questions should not be leading questions that incentivise respondents to prefer one answer over another.

---

### 5.2.4 Recruitment

#### 5.2.4.1 How do you find the people, your sample, to take your survey?

#### 5.2.4.2 Contact information and legality

If you obtained contact information from another source other than its originator, make sure it is consistent with any terms of use and any data protection laws.

---

### 5.2.5 Incentives and compensation

#### 5.2.5.1 Are you offering any incentives or compensation for survey takers?

If yes, provide details.

---

### 5.2.6 Anonymity

#### 5.2.6.1 Demographic section requirement

For the purpose of the course, the survey must have a demographic section containing facts about your survey takers, with questions about either:

- Age
- Gender
- Education
- Other relevant demographic information

#### 5.2.6.2 Email collection requirement

The survey must collect the respondent’s email address.  
The respondent’s name is not required.

---

### 5.2.7 Tools

#### 5.2.7.1 Provide the tools you are using

Include:

- Tools used to collect the data
- Tools used to analyze the data

---

### 5.2.8 Data Retention

#### 5.2.8.1 Handling identifiable data

If the data you collect can identify a respondent, the data should be kept in a separate and secure place with an anonymised code for each respondent.

---

### 5.2.9 Informed Consent

#### 5.2.9.1 No one should ever be compelled to complete your survey

#### 5.2.9.2 Voluntary completion as consent

The survey is completed voluntarily. This voluntary completion can be taken as consent for the data to be used in your research.

#### 5.2.9.3 Personal data notice

If personal data is collected, make sure respondents know that:

- Their data will not be published
- Their data will be destroyed after a given period  
  Suggested period: 6 months

---

### 5.2.10 Sampling, Response Rate and Selection

#### 5.2.10.1 Representativeness of results

If you are claiming that your survey results are applicable to a larger population beyond those who responded, meaning you have a representative sample, you must be able to justify that:

- Your recruitment database was genuinely representative
- Those who did not respond would have given the same answers

---

### 5.2.11 Analysis

#### 5.2.11.1 Closed questions

Closed questions can be used for statistical analysis, such as:

- Finding differences between groups within the sample
- Finding correlations between responses to questions

#### 5.2.11.2 Single-value statistics

Single-value statistics, such as “40% of respondents love this feature on our product,” have little research relevance unless you can compare that statistic to:

- Another external figure
- Another hypothesis

---

### 5.2.12 Conclusions based on survey result

#### 5.2.12.1 At the minimum, answer the following questions

##### 5.2.12.1.1 Demographic conclusion

Demographically, who are they?  
What does this mean to your startup, product, or service?

##### 5.2.12.1.2 Customer problems

What real problems are customers dealing with in your market?  
Why?  
How many of them meet the same problems?

##### 5.2.12.1.3 Customer interest

Are customers interested in your idea?  
Why and how?

##### 5.2.12.1.4 Willingness to pay

Are customers willing to pay to get their problem solved?  
Why and how?

##### 5.2.12.1.5 Other relevant questions

Answer any other questions you see fit.
